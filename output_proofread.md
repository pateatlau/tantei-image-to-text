# ETHICAL CONCERNS AND DILEMMAS IN CIVIL SERVICES

- An ethical dilemma arises from a situation that necessitates a choice between competing sets of principles.
- One principle is compromised no matter the choice.
- Dilemmas

  - Right vs. Right
  - Conjoint Dilemmas

# LAW
Laws refer to a set of codified norms that are enforced by the state to achieve the common good in society or the country.

- Thomas Aquinas' Division of Law
  - Natural Law - based on human nature
  - Positive Law - depends on the free will of legislators

- Contractarian Theory of Law
  - Exchange of Services
    - Citizens surrender power and liberty
    - The state provides security

# Civil Law
- A civil law is a kind of positive law that a legislator freely enacts and promulgates.
- It reinforces, determines/clarifies, and declares/codifies natural law, sometimes even beyond.

# Obeying Civil Law is based on the following assumptions:
1. There is a lawful government with proper authority
2. The laws are not contrary to natural law
3. It is morally possible to obey
4. They are for the common good
5. It has been sufficiently promulgated

* The argument favours civil disobedience in the absence of any condition.

# RULES
- Rules are more flexible and carry low-end consequences. Ethics supplements their implementation.
- E.g., Solid Waste Management Rules, 2016, provide guidelines for maintaining and disposing of waste in an environmentally friendly way.
- They are often instructions for a specific group/section that govern the actions of individuals on a daily basis.

# REGULATIONS
- They are a rigid set of rules authorised by legislation.
- They help clarify laws.

# CONSCIENCE
- Is a special act of the mind that comes into being when the intellect passes judgement on the goodness or badness of a particular act.
- (Deontologically), it is not a feeling or emotion, but an intellectual deciding being.
- Conscience lays down practical rules for specific actions,
- It applies the law or rule to specific actions.
- Laws and rules cannot be defined for every situation; thus, conscience acts as a source of ethical guidance in the absence of specific instructions.

# Features of Good Governance:
1. Participation:
    - Of all sections of society is the cornerstone of good governance.
    - It must be informed: freedom of association and organised civil society expression
    - E.g., 73rd and 74th Amendment Act, 1992.
2. Consensus-Oriented:
    - E.g., it requires mediation of different interests to reach a consensus on what is best for the whole community and how it can be achieved.
    - E.g., parliamentary sessions, NEST and NCSE, etc.
3. Rule of Law:
    - E.g., it requires a fair legal framework that puts law above everyone, and also for full protection of human rights (e.g., independent judiciary).
4. Equitable and Inclusive:
    - All members of society must benefit from the governance (e.g., MGNREGA, PDS, etc.).
    - (Welfare for all, especially vulnerable sections)
5. Accountable:
    - Acknowledgement and assumption of responsibility for actions, decisions, policies, etc.
    - E.g., grievance redressal mechanisms, tribunals, courts.
6. Responsive:
    - All stakeholders must be served within a time frame; timely delivery of services.
7. Transparency:
    - Free and accessible information on decisions and processes (e.g., Citizen's Charter, RTI).
8. Effective and Efficient:
    - Optimal use of resources
    - Producing results that meet the needs of the people.

# UTILISATION OF PUBLIC FUNDS
Public fund is money that is generated by the government to provide goods and services to the general public. It is held by the government as a custodian and not an owner.
- The capacity of the government to deliver its programmes, strengthen the economy, ensure security and rule of law, and preserve social cohesion, depends not just on the funds at its disposal, but on how they are used.
Principles to govern public utilisation of funds.
1. Only spent for public purpose.
2. Spent in accordance with the law (rule of law)
3. Outcome-driven and not input/output (budget planning).
  (e.g.) outcome: number of children enrolled should be a criterion rather than input: amount of money invested into school.
4. Criteria and decision-making process (criteria in the decision planning process) to be transparent and available to the public.
5. Timely release of funds (avoid March rush).
6. Inclusive in nature → most marginalised given top priority (prioritisation).

   - d) multiple layers and procedures, from sanctioning authorities to executing agents.
   - e) delay in release of funds results in 'March rush' where the agents then try to spend a large portion of funds indiscriminately so that they do not lapse at the end of the year.

# Self-Help Groups (SHGs)
*   Small, informal groups typically composed of 10-20 members who come together to address common issues and improve economic conditions.

## Functioning
1.  **Formation and Meetings**
    *   within a community (facilitated by NGOs or government).
    *   Meetings are regular to discuss activities.
2.  **Savings and Credit**
    *   Members pool small amounts into a group fund.
    *   Savings are lent to members for purposes like business, education, or emergencies.
3.  **Decision-making**
    *   Democratic process of decision-making with proper record-keeping.
4.  **Linkage with Banks**
    *   to access larger collective loans.
    *   They have high creditworthiness and a bank repayment rate (SHG is 96%).
5.  **Capacity Building** - SHGs also support members through education, training, skill development, financial literacy, etc., received from NGOs, banks, and government agencies.

    *   Swarnajayanti Gram Swarozgar Yojana (SGSY).

# Co-operatives
*   Autonomous associations of individuals who voluntarily come together to meet their common economic, social, and cultural needs and aspirations through a jointly owned and democratically controlled enterprise.

## Functioning
1.  Open membership and democratic control.
2.  Equitable contribution to capital.
3.  Proportional benefits from surpluses/profits.
4.  Autonomy and self-governance.
5.  Education and training of members.

# Co-operatives in India
*   began in the early 20th century to address issues of rural credit and support farmers.
*   Government promoted cooperatives after independence as a means of socio-economic development.
*   National Cooperative Development Corporation (NCDC) was set up.

## Constitutional Provisions
*   Article 19: Right to form co-operatives.
*   97th Constitutional Amendment: Article 43B: under DPSP to promote co-operatives.
    *   Part IXB - dealing with co-operatives; outlining composition, election, principles, etc.

## Legal Framework
*   Multi-State Cooperative Societies Act and Rules, 2002 (Amendment)
    *   for MSCS that operate in multiple states.
*   State Cooperative Societies Act - for each state.
